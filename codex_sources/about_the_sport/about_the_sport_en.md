## What is BASE Jumping?

BASE jumping is the practice of parachuting from fixed objects rather than aircraft. The acronym BASE stands for:

* Building
* Antenna
* Span (bridges)
* Earth (cliffs and natural formations)

Unlike skydiving, where jumps typically begin thousands of meters above the ground, BASE jumps often start from much lower altitudes. This leaves little time for problem solving and significantly reduces the margin for error.

Modern BASE jumping emerged in the late 1970s and has since developed into a small but globally connected community. Today, participants jump from urban structures, bridges, mountains, sea cliffs, and other fixed objects around the world.

For many participants, BASE jumping is not simply a sport. It is often described as a combination of adventure, technical skill, environmental awareness, personal challenge, and community. Some are drawn by the opportunity to explore remote landscapes, others by the precision and discipline required to operate safely in a high consequence environment.

At the same time, BASE jumping remains one of the most dangerous recreational activities in the world. Even experienced athletes can be seriously injured or killed. As a result, successful participation requires extensive preparation, progressive learning, sound judgment, and a deep understanding of risk.

This documentary explores BASE jumping as a social and cultural phenomenon. Rather than teaching how to jump, it aims to provide insight into the people, motivations, values, and decisions that shape the sport today.

## History of BASE Jumping

Although people have experimented with parachutes from towers, cliffs, and balloons for centuries, modern BASE jumping is generally traced back to the late 1970s.

A key figure in the sport's development was Carl Boenish, an American skydiver, filmmaker, and pioneer who helped transform isolated fixed object jumps into a recognised activity. In 1978, Boenish and several fellow jumpers completed a series of parachute jumps from El Capitan in Yosemite National Park using modern ram air parachutes. These jumps are often regarded as the beginning of modern BASE jumping.

Boenish also introduced the acronym BASE, representing the four categories of objects from which jumps are made: Building, Antenna, Span, and Earth. To encourage exploration and progression, he established the BASE Number system. Participants who completed at least one jump from each object category could apply for an official number, a tradition that continues today.

Throughout the 1980s and 1990s, the sport remained relatively small. Information was passed primarily through personal networks, mentorship, and direct experience. Access to equipment, knowledge, and experienced jumpers was limited, creating a culture that emphasised patience, commitment, and learning from others.

The arrival of the internet significantly changed the sport. Online forums, digital cameras, video platforms, and social media made BASE jumping more visible than ever before. Knowledge became easier to access, but so did dramatic imagery that often highlighted spectacular moments without showing the preparation behind them.

Today, BASE jumping is practiced on every inhabited continent. While participation remains small compared to mainstream sports, technological developments, global travel, and digital media have connected athletes across the world and accelerated the exchange of knowledge, ideas, and experiences.

At the same time, many experienced jumpers continue to emphasise that the fundamental challenges of the sport have not changed. Gravity, weather, terrain, and human decision making remain the defining factors that shape every jump.

## Difference Between Skydiving and BASE Jumping

At first glance, skydiving and BASE jumping may appear similar. Both involve exiting a platform, experiencing freefall, and deploying a parachute. In practice, however, they differ significantly in terms of environment, equipment, risk, and decision making.

Skydiving takes place from aircraft, typically at altitudes between 3,000 and 4,000 metres above ground. This provides substantial time to solve problems, recover from instability, and react to equipment malfunctions. Modern skydiving systems also include backup equipment such as reserve parachutes and automatic activation devices.

BASE jumps are usually performed from much lower altitudes. Depending on the object, a jumper may have only a few seconds between exit and parachute deployment. Because there is often insufficient altitude for a reserve parachute to function effectively, most BASE systems use a single parachute specifically designed for the demands of fixed object jumping.

The environment also differs considerably. Skydivers generally operate in open airspace with large landing areas and minimal obstacles. BASE jumpers frequently deal with cliffs, buildings, bridges, power lines, trees, uneven terrain, changing winds, and restricted landing zones. As a result, navigation, judgement, and environmental awareness become critical.

For these reasons, skydiving is widely regarded as the foundation for BASE jumping. Many athletes spend years developing canopy control, freefall skills, weather awareness, and emergency procedures before transitioning to fixed object jumping.

A common misconception is that BASE jumping is simply a more extreme version of skydiving. While the two activities share technical roots, experienced participants often view them as fundamentally different disciplines. In many ways, BASE jumping is less about freefall and more about preparation, decision making, risk management, and operating safely in complex environments.

## Equipment

BASE jumping uses specialised equipment designed for fixed object environments and low altitude deployments. Although many components resemble those used in skydiving, the systems are optimised for different conditions and priorities.

The most visible component is the parachute system. Unlike modern skydiving rigs, which typically contain both a main and reserve parachute, most BASE systems contain a single parachute specifically designed for reliable openings at low altitudes. These canopies are generally larger, more stable, and built to open quickly and predictably.

Another key component is the pilot chute, a small auxiliary parachute used to extract the main canopy from the container. Depending on the jump, the pilot chute may be handheld, stowed, or assisted through various deployment methods.

The container itself is designed to be lightweight and streamlined while remaining durable enough for use in demanding environments. Many systems are customised to suit different disciplines and levels of experience.

Protective equipment is equally important. Helmets are standard throughout the sport, and many jumpers use additional protection such as back protectors, knee pads, ankle support, or impact resistant clothing depending on the terrain and type of jump.

For jumps involving extended freefall, athletes may use tracking suits or wingsuits. These garments increase aerodynamic performance and allow jumpers to travel greater horizontal distances before deploying their parachute. However, they also introduce additional complexity and risk.

Despite advances in equipment design, experienced athletes consistently emphasise that technology alone does not create safety. Equipment can reduce certain risks, but it cannot compensate for poor judgement, inadequate preparation, or a lack of experience. In BASE jumping, decision making remains the most important piece of equipment a person carries.

## Risk and Safety Culture

BASE jumping is widely regarded as one of the highest risk recreational activities in the world. Unlike many other sports, mistakes often occur in environments where there is little time, altitude, or space to recover. As a result, safety culture occupies a central place within the community.

Contrary to popular stereotypes, experienced BASE jumpers generally do not view risk as something to be ignored or glorified. Instead, much of the sport revolves around understanding, evaluating, and managing risk. Decisions about weather, equipment, physical condition, terrain, and personal readiness often determine whether a jump takes place at all.

Many athletes spend significantly more time preparing for a jump than performing it. Site assessments, weather analysis, equipment checks, route planning, and discussions with experienced peers are common parts of the process. In many cases, choosing not to jump is considered just as important as choosing to jump.

A recurring theme within the sport is progression. Athletes are encouraged to develop skills gradually, build experience over time, and avoid advancing faster than their abilities allow. Many incidents have historically been linked not only to technical failures, but also to poor judgement, overconfidence, complacency, or insufficient preparation.

Mentorship also plays an important role. Although training pathways vary between countries and communities, experienced jumpers often pass on knowledge, local expertise, and lessons learned from previous incidents. This informal transfer of knowledge remains one of the defining characteristics of the sport.

While modern equipment and technology have improved reliability, they have not eliminated the fundamental risks of BASE jumping. Ultimately, safety depends on the ability to make informed decisions, recognise limitations, and exercise restraint when conditions are not suitable.

## Community and Ethics

Despite its global reach, BASE jumping remains a relatively small community. Many participants describe the sport not only through the jumps themselves, but through the relationships, shared experiences, and collective responsibility that develop around it.

Historically, knowledge within the sport was passed primarily through personal mentorship and direct experience. Before online resources became widely available, newcomers often relied on experienced jumpers to learn local practices, understand site specific challenges, and develop safe habits. While information is now more accessible than ever, personal guidance and community knowledge continue to play an important role.

Alongside technical skills, many participants place significant value on ethics. Although there is no single governing body overseeing BASE jumping worldwide, informal norms and expectations have emerged within the community over decades.

These often include:

* Respecting local communities and landowners
* Protecting access to established jump sites
* Avoiding behaviour that may endanger others
* Supporting less experienced jumpers
* Sharing accurate information
* Taking responsibility for personal decisions

Because access to many jump locations depends on local relationships and mutual trust, individual actions can affect opportunities for others. For this reason, behaviour away from the exit point is often considered just as important as behaviour at the exit point.

The growth of digital media has introduced new opportunities and challenges. Athletes can now share experiences instantly with global audiences, connect with others across continents, and contribute to the visibility of the sport. At the same time, questions have emerged around representation, responsibility, and the difference between documenting an activity and influencing how it is perceived.

As with many small communities, opinions often differ. Yet across different countries, disciplines, and generations, a common theme remains: the long term future of the sport depends not only on individual skill, but also on how participants treat one another, their environment, and the wider public.

## Disciplines of BASE Jumping

Although BASE jumping is often portrayed as a single activity, it encompasses a variety of disciplines that differ in technique, equipment, objectives, and environment. Most athletes specialise in certain disciplines over time, while others move between them depending on their interests and experience.

### Traditional BASE Jumping

Traditional BASE jumping is the foundation of the sport. The objective is straightforward: exit a fixed object, maintain stability during freefall, and deploy the parachute safely before landing.

Depending on the height of the object, freefall may last only a few seconds or considerably longer. Traditional jumps place strong emphasis on preparation, canopy control, and decision making rather than aerial performance.

### Tracking

Tracking is a form of human flight that uses body position or specialised tracking suits to increase horizontal movement during freefall.

Rather than falling primarily downward, athletes aim to move away from the object while maximising distance travelled. Tracking requires a strong understanding of aerodynamics, body control, and terrain awareness, and is often viewed as an intermediate step between traditional BASE jumping and wingsuit flying.

### Wingsuit BASE Jumping

Wingsuit BASE jumping combines a wingsuit with a BASE parachute system. The wingsuit creates lift, allowing athletes to glide significant distances before deploying their parachute.

Today, wingsuit flying is one of the most visible disciplines within the sport and has shaped public perceptions of BASE jumping through documentaries, films, and social media. It is also considered one of the most technically demanding forms of the sport and typically requires years of prior experience.

### Aerials and Acrobatics

Some athletes perform aerial manoeuvres during freefall, including flips, spins, and other acrobatic movements before deploying their parachute.

These jumps require precise timing, body awareness, and altitude management. While acrobatic jumps have been part of BASE jumping for decades, they generally represent a small and specialised area of the sport.

### Low Object Jumping

Not all BASE jumps involve extended freefall. Jumps from very low objects often require specialised deployment techniques designed to ensure immediate parachute inflation after exit.

Because there is little time available to react, these jumps place particular emphasis on preparation, equipment configuration, and precision.

### Multi Way Jumps

Multi way jumps involve two or more athletes exiting together and coordinating their movements during freefall.

Participants may fly planned formations, perform synchronised manoeuvres, or simply share the experience of a jump. As with many group activities in aviation and parachuting, communication, planning, and trust play a critical role.

### A Diverse Sport

While public attention often focuses on wingsuit flying, it represents only one part of a much broader sport. From low bridge jumps to alpine wingsuit flights, BASE jumping encompasses a wide range of disciplines, environments, and personal approaches.

What unites them is the combination of preparation, technical skill, risk management, and the unique experience of human flight from fixed objects.
## Modern Developments

Over the past two decades, BASE jumping has undergone significant changes. Advances in equipment, communication technologies, and digital media have transformed how athletes learn, connect, and share their experiences.

The internet has made information more accessible than ever before. Training resources, weather data, mapping tools, and communication platforms allow participants to exchange knowledge across countries and continents. Athletes who once relied primarily on local networks can now learn from a global community.

At the same time, technological developments have improved many aspects of the sport. Modern parachute systems, specialised protective equipment, navigation tools, action cameras, and weather forecasting technologies have increased both capability and access to information.

Social media has played a particularly influential role. Platforms such as Instagram, YouTube, and other video sharing services have brought BASE jumping to audiences far beyond the sport itself. Spectacular images and flight footage have contributed to growing public awareness and have helped document achievements, locations, and personal stories.

However, increased visibility has also created new discussions within the community. Questions surrounding representation, responsibility, privacy, sponsorship, and public perception have become increasingly relevant. Athletes, filmmakers, brands, and audiences now participate in a constantly connected media environment that did not exist during the sport's early decades.

Despite these developments, many experienced participants argue that the core principles of BASE jumping remain unchanged. Sound judgement, preparation, humility, and respect for risk continue to be viewed as more important than technology, popularity, or visibility.

As the sport continues to evolve, it remains shaped by an ongoing balance between innovation and tradition, individual freedom and collective responsibility, exploration and restraint.
